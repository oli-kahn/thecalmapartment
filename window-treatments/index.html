No-Drill Window Treatments for Renters

Complete privacy and light control — without a single hole in the wall, one trip to the hardware store, or an awkward conversation with your landlord.

By The Calm Apartment · Updated May 2026 · 6 min read

Window treatments are one of the first things you notice in a room — and one of the last things renters feel they can change. Most leases explicitly forbid drilling into window frames, and curtain hardware feels permanent in a way that a Command hook doesn't.

But living without proper window coverings isn't an option either. You need privacy at night, light control for sleep, and something that looks intentional — not like you just moved in and never finished unpacking.

These six methods cover every window situation a renter might face. Each one installs without drills, removes without damage, and actually looks good while it's up.

What's in this guide

  1. Tension Rods
  2. Command Hook Curtains
  3. Adhesive Blinds
  4. Magnetic Coverings
  5. Twist-and-Fit Shades
  6. Privacy Film

1Tension Rods

The original renter hack — and still the best for most windows.

How it works:
  • A spring-loaded rod expands to fit inside the window frame recess
  • Rubber caps on both ends grip the frame without adhesives or hardware
  • Holds lightweight to medium-weight curtains — sheer panels, linen drapes, or blackout curtains up to about 5 lbs
  • Works on any window with a recessed frame deep enough to seat the rod (most standard windows)
  • Available in adjustable widths from 18" to over 80"
Why it's renter-safe: Zero contact with walls. The rod presses only against the inside of the window frame — a surface designed to handle contact. When you move out, twist to release tension and pull it down. Nothing left behind.

2Command Hook Curtains

When there's no recessed frame, Command hooks step in.

How it works:
  • Large Command hooks (the outdoor or jumbo size) attach to the wall above the window
  • A lightweight curtain rod rests across two or three hooks like a cradle
  • Best with slim, lightweight rods — think thin metal or bamboo, under 3 lbs total
  • Works on smooth painted walls, tile, and most non-textured surfaces
  • Remove by pulling the adhesive strip straight down — the tab stretches and releases cleanly
Why it's renter-safe: Command strips are engineered for clean removal. The adhesive stretches off when pulled correctly — no scraping, no residue, no paint peeling. Follow the weight rating (typically 5–7.5 lbs per hook) and you're golden. For extra security on heavier curtains, use one hook every 24" along the rod.

3Adhesive Blinds

No brackets, no screws — just stick and go.

How it works:
  • Pre-assembled blinds with adhesive strips built into the mounting bracket
  • Available in cellular (honeycomb), roller, and mini-blind styles
  • Stick directly to the top of the window frame or the wall above it
  • Cordless lift mechanisms are standard — safer and cleaner-looking
  • Most are cut-to-width or come in standard window sizes
Why it's renter-safe: The adhesive is factory-applied and designed specifically for blinds — not a DIY hack. Brands like Redi Shade and Blinds.com offer "no tools" lines that peel off when you're done. As with any adhesive product, test a small patch first if your walls have older paint or unique finishes.

4Magnetic Coverings

The steel-door special — instant, invisible, and surprisingly elegant.

How it works:
  • Strong magnets clamp fabric or lightweight blinds directly to a steel door or window frame
  • Works on metal entry doors, steel-framed windows, and some older apartment radiators
  • Magnetic curtain rods exist — or DIY with neodymium magnets and a lightweight rod
  • Zero installation — place the magnets, hang the rod or fabric, done
  • Ideal for door windows, sidelights, and small bathroom windows with metal frames
Why it's renter-safe: Magnets don't leave a trace. No adhesive, no tension, no friction — just magnetism. The moment you pull them off, the surface is exactly as it was. This is the only method that works on metal surfaces where adhesives struggle and drilling is impossible.

5Twist-and-Fit Shades

Spring-loaded shades that expand sideways into the frame — like a tension rod, but for blinds.

How it works:
  • A spring mechanism inside the shade pushes outward against both sides of the window frame
  • Twist to compress, position inside the frame, release to expand and lock in place
  • Typically cellular or pleated fabric — provides light filtering and some insulation
  • No cutting, no measuring beyond width — they self-adjust to fit
  • Available in light-filtering and blackout options
Why it's renter-safe: Like tension rods, twist-and-fit shades grip only the inside of the window frame. The rubberized ends prevent scratching. They stay up through friction alone — no screws, no adhesive, no modification to the property. Compress and remove in seconds on move-out day.

6Privacy Film

The invisible solution — turns any window into frosted glass.

How it works:
  • Static-cling or adhesive film applies directly to the glass with water and a squeegee
  • Static-cling versions use no glue — they stick via surface tension and peel off in one sheet
  • Patterns range from simple frosted to rain glass, rice paper, and stained-glass effects
  • Blocks the view in while letting light through — perfect for street-facing and bathroom windows
  • Also available in UV-blocking and heat-reducing varieties for sun-facing windows
Why it's renter-safe: Static-cling film is the gold standard — zero adhesive, zero residue. It clings to the glass with water and peels off like a sticker when you're done. Even adhesive films (applied with soapy water) remove cleanly from glass surfaces. The glass itself is untouched — no scratches, no film, no evidence anyone was ever there.

Pick your method based on your window

Recessed frame with depth? Tension rods or twist-and-fit shades. Flat wall above? Command hooks. Metal door? Magnets. Street-facing glass you never want to look through? Privacy film. Most apartments will use a combination — film on the bathroom window, tension rods in the bedroom, magnets on the entry door.
