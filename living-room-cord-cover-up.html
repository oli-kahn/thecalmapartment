8 No-Drill Ways to Hide Ugly Cords in a Rental Living Room

Tame your cable chaos without a single hole in the wall. Everything peels off when you move.

Rental living rooms collect cords. The TV, the router, the lamp, the phone charger, the speaker. Each one adds another black wire snaking across the floor or dangling down the wall. Drilling holes to route them through the drywall is not an option, and most cable management advice assumes you own the place. These eight methods use adhesive clips, fabric sleeves, magnetic holders, and behind-furniture routing to make every cord disappear. Nothing requires a drill. Everything comes off clean when your lease is up.

01

Adhesive cord clips along the baseboard

Run adhesive cord clips along the top edge of your baseboard and snap cables into them one at a time. The clips come in clear or white plastic and each one holds a single cord (ethernet, lamp wire, phone charger) in a straight line flush against the trim. Stick them every twelve to eighteen inches along the baseboard route. When your lease ends, pull each clip off with your fingers; the adhesive releases without marking the baseboard paint.

Sticks to baseboard trim. Pulls off by hand. Nothing touches the wall itself.
02

Fabric cord covers that zip around cable bundles

Fabric cord covers are zippered sleeves made from cotton or linen-blend fabric that wrap around a bundle of cables and turn them into something that reads as intentional rather than chaotic. Slide your TV, router, and console cables inside, zip it shut, and the whole cluster becomes one textile tube instead of a nest of black wires. They come in lengths from three to ten feet and colors that match common wall paints like beige, gray, and cream. Unzip, pull the cables out, and toss the cover in your moving box on the way out.

Zero wall contact. Reusable in your next apartment. Takes thirty seconds to install or remove.
03

Braided cable sleeves that expand and contract

Braided polyethylene sleeves expand when you push them open, wrap around multiple cords, then contract to hold everything tight in one tidy bundle. Cut the sleeve to length with scissors, push a handful of cables through, and the braid hugs them into a single organized line. The material flexes around corners and behind desks without kinking or bunching. When you move, slide the sleeve off and reuse it. The braid does not lose tension after repeated use.

Touches only the cables, not the walls or floor. Reusable. Costs about eight dollars for a ten-foot length.
04

Decorative cord concealers shaped like wall art

Decorative cord concealers are small channels shaped like branches, vines, or geometric lines that mount on the wall with adhesive backing and hide a single cord inside. They turn a hanging lamp cord into something that looks like intentional wall decor instead of an electrical hazard. Stick one above a plug, run it vertically to the device, and the cord disappears into the channel. Pull it off with gentle heat from a hairdryer when you move; the adhesive releases without lifting paint.

Adhesive peels with hairdryer heat. The wall underneath stays paint-intact.
05

Route everything behind furniture

Route every cable behind your media console, sofa, or bookcase so nothing crosses open floor space. Bundle cords with velcro ties at the back of each piece of furniture, then run a single flat extension cord to the nearest outlet. If the outlet is on the opposite wall, use a flat-profile extension cord and slide it under a low-pile area rug. The rug covers it completely and you cannot feel it underfoot. Zero adhesives, zero wall contact, nothing to clean up when you move.

No products needed beyond what you already own. Gravity and furniture placement do the work.
06

Peel-and-stick cord channels you can paint to match the wall

Peel-and-stick cord channels are rigid PVC tracks that stick to the wall with pre-applied adhesive and snap shut over your cables. Cut them to length with a hacksaw, paint them to match your wall color if you want them to disappear, and press them in place along the baseboard or up to a wall-mounted TV. The front cover snaps open for adding or removing cables later without peeling the channel off. Pry them off when you move; any leftover adhesive rubs away with isopropyl alcohol on a rag.

Paintable to blend into the wall. Adhesive residue cleans up with alcohol. No drywall damage.
07

Hide the power strip inside a decorative box

Hide a bulky power strip and all its plugged-in cords inside a lidded decorative box. Find a box large enough to fit your power strip flat (about 12 by 6 inches inside) and use a utility knife to cut a notch in the back edge for the cords to pass through. The box sits on the floor next to the outlet, looking like a decorative object rather than a utility hub. No wall contact at all. When you move, lift the box and take it with you.

Zero installation. The box sits on the floor and comes with you. No landlord involvement whatsoever.
08

Magnetic cable clips on steel furniture legs and surfaces

Magnetic cable clips and ties attach cords to any steel surface without adhesive: the side of a metal desk leg, a filing cabinet, a metal bed frame, a radiator cover. The magnet holds the clip in place, you route the cord through it, and it stays put even when you tug the cord to reposition a device. This works on steel furniture legs, metal shelving units, and refrigerator sides. Pull the magnet off by hand when you want to change the route or when you move out. No residue, no tools, nothing to peel or scrape.

Magnets pull off by hand. Zero residue. Only works on steel. Test with a fridge magnet first.
