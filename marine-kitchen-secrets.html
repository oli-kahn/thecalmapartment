8 Small-Space Solutions Borrowed from Boats

Galley tricks that work in your kitchen. No marina required.

Boat galleys solve the same problems your rental kitchen has: limited counter space, minimal storage, and surfaces that don't belong to you. These eight ideas borrow from marine design but adapt for dry land. Everything installs without permanent changes to walls, cabinets, or tile.

01

Mount an under-cabinet wineglass rack

Boat galleys fit stemware racks under upper cabinets because every inch counts when the hull is 28 feet long. The same rack works in your kitchen. It mounts under a wall cabinet with short screws into the cabinet bottom, keeping wine glasses and champagne flutes off the counter and out of the cabinet where they clink together every time you open the door. Most racks hold six to nine glasses by the base in a single row. Measure the stem diameter of your glasses before ordering; flared stems on some flutes need wider slots. If you can't screw into the cabinet, look for adhesive-backed versions with heavy-duty 3M strips rated for the weight of your glassware.

Screws into the cabinet underside, not the wall. Fill the tiny holes with wood filler on move-out day and nobody sees them.
02

Stick a magnetic knife strip to your backsplash

Galley kitchens on sailboats use magnetic strips on the bulkhead because drawers slide open in a swell and knife blocks topple over. A wall-mounted magnetic knife strip gives you the same hands-free access in a small kitchen. Most come in stainless steel or wood-backed versions, 12 to 18 inches long, with rare-earth magnets strong enough to hold chef's knives, kitchen shears, and a steel spatula. Stick it to a tile backsplash with the included adhesive tape, or screw it into the side of an upper cabinet if you want something you can later fill. Keep the strip away from your stove; grease and steam coat the magnets over time and weaken the grip.

Adhesive-backed versions stick to tile backsplashes and peel off clean with heat from a hairdryer.
03

Add a fold-down wall table for instant counter space

Sailboats use fold-down chart tables that swing up against the bulkhead when not in use. A wall-mounted drop-leaf shelf gives your kitchen the same trick: extra counter space for meal prep that disappears when you need the floor back. Look for a solid wood or bamboo bracket shelf, 24 to 30 inches wide, with folding hinges rated for 50 pounds or more. Mount it at counter height on a clear stretch of wall, or attach it to the side of a cabinet if wall studs are hard to find. Lock it in the upright position with a simple barrel bolt when you're not using it. For a fully removable install, there are models with heavy-duty adhesive mounting plates instead of screws.

Use adhesive mounting plates for a no-drill install. Even screwed versions leave only four small holes to fill on move-out.
04

Repurpose a teak wall caddy for spice jars

Boat chart tables often have a teak caddy mounted nearby for dividers, pencils, and plotting tools. The same compact organizer, repurposed for your backsplash, holds spice jars, oil bottles, and the cooking utensils you reach for every day. Teak handles kitchen moisture better than most woods; it's the same timber used on boat decks. Look for a three-compartment caddy with a flat back, about 12 to 16 inches wide. Mount it with heavy-duty Command strips rated for the weight of your jars. If your backsplash is tile, clean it with rubbing alcohol before sticking anything on so the adhesive bonds properly to the surface.

Command strips hold it to tile or painted drywall. Peel off without damaging grout or paint.
05

Use marine-grade suction hooks on tile and glass

Industrial suction cups used on boat decks to hold fenders and lines grip surfaces far better than your average bathroom suction hook. In your kitchen, a marine-grade suction hook on a glass or tile backsplash holds oven mitts, a dish towel, measuring spoons on a ring, or a small colander. The lever-activated kind creates vacuum pressure that holds 10 to 15 pounds when properly seated. Press hard against a clean, smooth surface, flip the lever, and the hook locks in place. They only work on non-porous surfaces, so skip them on textured tile, painted drywall, or wood. A set of three gives you hanging storage across the entire backsplash without a single hole.

Pure vacuum suction. No adhesive, no residue, no mark on tile or glass. Relocates in seconds.
06

Lean a boat ladder rack against the wall for towels

A leaning ladder rack borrows from the compact towel storage on boats where wall space is taken up by portholes and cabinetry. In your kitchen, a wooden ladder leaned against the wall holds dish towels, an apron, a market tote, and a couple of lightweight cutting boards without a single screw. Look for one with flat rungs at least 2 inches wide so towels don't slip off. Rubber feet at the bottom keep it from sliding on tile or wood floors, and a simple strap or clip near the top secures it to the wall if you have kids or a dog that might knock it. Alder, bamboo, and pine versions all work; stain or paint the wood to match your kitchen.

Freestanding. Leans against the wall. No holes, no brackets. Picks up and moves with you.
07

Hang a brass porthole mirror with Command strips

Porthole mirrors, the round brass-framed kind you see on cabin doors, add depth and bounce light around a small kitchen the same way they brighten tight boat interiors. Hang one on a blank kitchen wall with large Command picture-hanging strips and you get the look of a window without cutting through drywall. A 12 to 18 inch diameter mirror is enough to reflect the light from your range hood or under-cabinet fixture and make the room feel wider. Brass or antique gold frames warm up white rental kitchens instantly. The mirror stays up as long as you want and comes off with a pull of the strip tab, leaving no residue on the paint.

Command strips hold the weight and peel off clean. The wall looks exactly as it did before.
08

Organize your pantry with stackable nesting containers

Boat provisioning boxes nest inside each other when empty and stack securely when full; the same design solves pantry chaos in a rental kitchen. A set of nesting storage containers with airtight lids keeps flour, sugar, pasta, rice, cereal, and snacks organized without wasting shelf space. When you run low on something, the empty container nests inside the next size and disappears. Look for BPA-free plastic or stainless steel sets with clear lids so you can see what's inside at a glance. Square or rectangular shapes use shelf space more efficiently than round canisters. Stack three high on a pantry shelf and label the front of each one with masking tape and a marker so you know what's where without pulling everything out.

Freestanding. Sits on pantry shelves. Packs flat and moves to your next kitchen.
