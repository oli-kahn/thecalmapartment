8 No-Drill Furniture Upgrades Every Renter Can Do This Weekend

Your landlord said no holes. Your furniture didn't get the memo. Here's how to give every piece a facelift with nothing but peel-and-stick products and a free afternoon.

By The Calm Apartment · Updated June 2026 · 7 min read

Rental furniture has a look. You know the one — flat-packed bookcases with that unmistakable white laminate sheen, a dresser that's been through three roommates, a sofa in a color you'd never pick if you had a choice. Short of buying all-new furniture (which you can't haul up three flights of stairs every time you move), most renters just live with it.

These eight upgrades change that math. Every single one sticks on with adhesive, wraps around with fabric, or presses into place without touching a drill. They come off clean when the lease ends, and they turn furniture you tolerate into furniture you actually like looking at.

1Adhesive Scalloped Trim on Bookcase Edges

$12–$25 per roll

IKEA bookcases are functional and aggressively plain. Peel-and-stick scalloped trim — the kind that comes on a roll with pre-applied double-sided tape — turns those straight shelf edges into something with actual personality. You cut it to length with scissors, press it along the front edge of each shelf, and suddenly a $79 Billy looks like it came from a vintage shop.

What to look for:
  • Flexible PVC or foam trim — bends around corners without cracking
  • Pre-applied adhesive backing, not the kind you have to glue separately
  • Scalloped or wave patterns read as intentional detail, not craft-project
  • White or cream blends into most laminate finishes seamlessly

Works on: bookcases, media consoles, floating shelves, the top edge of a plain desk. The trim peels off with a little heat from a hairdryer when you move — no gouges, no sticky ghost left behind.

2Contact Paper Drawer Fronts

$8–$20 per roll

A dresser with sad, scratched-up drawer fronts can turn into a statement piece with one roll of adhesive contact paper. Cut a rectangle the size of each drawer face, peel the backing, smooth it on from the center outward, and trim the edges with a craft knife. The paper sits flush — no bubbles if you take your time — and the dresser reads as a completely different piece.

What to look for:
  • Matte finish — glossy contact paper screams "I covered this with plastic"
  • Wood-grain or solid neutral tones age better than bold patterns
  • Removable adhesive label on the packaging (some are semi-permanent)
  • At least 18 inches wide so you can cover a drawer in one piece

Good for: dresser drawers, desk drawers, IKEA Malm and Hemnes fronts, the flat side panels of a laminate nightstand. Removal is the same drill — hairdryer on low for thirty seconds and the whole sheet lifts off.

3Peel-and-Stick Furniture Molding

$10–$30 per kit

Flat-front cabinets and sideboards are the default in affordable furniture. Peel-and-stick molding strips — thin PVC or foam rails with adhesive already on the back — let you fake the look of paneled doors without touching a saw or a nail gun. You stick them in a rectangular pattern on each cabinet face, and suddenly the piece reads as built-in rather than big-box.

What to look for:
  • Flexible material that cuts with scissors — rigid strips won't sit flat
  • Paintable surface so you can match the cabinet color exactly
  • Profile depth of at least ⅛ inch to cast a visible shadow line
  • 3M or similar adhesive rated for painted MDF and laminate

Works on: IKEA Bestå sideboards, plain kitchen cabinet fronts (if your landlord didn't install them), flat wardrobe doors, the front of a media console. It all comes off with gentle heat and a plastic scraper — zero evidence left behind.

4Stick-On Furniture Legs

$15–$35 set of 4

A lot of budget furniture sits low to the ground — IKEA Kallax units, flat-pack TV stands, those cube organizers everyone owns. Adhesive furniture legs (basically short tapered feet with a heavy-duty sticky pad on top) lift the whole piece by 3 to 6 inches. The gap underneath makes the room feel less bottom-heavy, and you suddenly have clearance for a robot vacuum to actually do its job.

What to look for:
  • Weight rating per leg — four legs rated for 50 lbs each can hold 200 lbs total
  • Wide adhesive base (at least 2 inches across) for stability on laminate
  • Tapered or mid-century profile — blocky risers look like an afterthought
  • Rubber foot cap on the bottom protects hard floors from scratches

Good for: low media consoles, cube storage units, a coffee table that sits too close to the floor, nightstands that feel squat. When you move, twist the legs off slowly — the adhesive releases without peeling the laminate, especially if you warm it first.

5No-Drill Handle Upgrades

$8–$20 set of 4

The hardware that comes on rental furniture is almost always the cheapest thing the manufacturer could legally attach — thin metal bars, plastic knobs that wobble, or handles shaped like nothing in particular. Adhesive cabinet knobs and pulls let you swap them out without drilling new holes. The knob has a flat adhesive back that sticks directly onto the drawer or door face, right over the old hardware spot if you popped the original off.

What to look for:
  • Stainless steel or brass — plastic adhesive knobs look like they belong in a dorm
  • Clear acrylic base plate distributes the pull force across more surface area
  • Diameter at least 1 inch — tiny knobs look lost on full-size drawers
  • Pull strength rated for 10+ lbs if it's a drawer you actually open daily

Works on: dresser drawers, kitchen cabinets (if removable), wardrobe doors, nightstand drawers. To take them off later, slide dental floss behind the base plate to cut through the adhesive — no residue, no peeled finish.

6Removable Wallpaper Furniture Backs

$15–$40 per roll

Open bookcases show the wall behind them — usually beige, usually boring. Peel-and-stick wallpaper applied to the back panel (the thin board that forms the rear of each cubby) adds a shot of pattern that's only visible through the shelves. It's one of those details guests notice without being able to name, and it makes the whole bookcase feel custom.

What to look for:
  • Peel-and-stick, not paste-applied — no water, no mess, no dry time
  • Muted pattern (small-scale botanical, tonal stripe, textured linen look)
  • Easy to trim with scissors or a craft knife for precise cubby sizing
  • Removable without residue — test a corner after a month to be sure

Works on: IKEA Kallax cubbies, Billy bookcase backs, the inside back panel of a glass-front cabinet, any open shelving unit. When you move, pull it off in sheets — the thin backing board underneath won't show any trace.

7Washi Tape Furniture Accents

$6–$15 per set

Washi tape is basically fancy masking tape made from rice paper — low-tack, easy to reposition, and it comes in enough patterns to make your head spin. Run a strip along the front edge of a shelf, frame a drawer with a thin border, or create a geometric pattern across the flat side of a cabinet. It costs less than a coffee and you can change it whenever the mood strikes.

What to look for:
  • Wide rolls (15mm+) for borders — narrow tape gets lost on furniture
  • Metallic or solid colors read as intentional; character prints read as craft
  • Low-tack paper tape, not plastic — real washi pulls off without a trace
  • Multi-roll sets give you options without buying ten individual rolls

Works on: drawer edges, shelf fronts, the perimeter of a mirror frame, lamp bases, picture frame borders, anywhere a thin line of color adds definition. It lifts off with just your fingernail — no heat, no tools, no sorry-landlord text.

8Furniture Slipcovers

$25–$60 per cover

A sofa in landlord-beige or a chair in a color someone else picked ten years ago can change completely under a stretch slipcover. The good ones are made from jersey knit or velvet-feel stretch fabric that hugs the contours of the piece instead of looking like a draped sheet. You tuck the extra fabric into the crevices between seat and arm, and from across the room it reads as upholstery.

What to look for:
  • Stretch jersey or velvet-feel fabric — woven cotton slipcovers wrinkle and shift
  • Elastic hem around the bottom that grips the base of the furniture
  • Separate cushion covers included if your sofa has removable cushions
  • Machine-washable — spills happen and you want to throw it in the laundry

Works on: sofas, armchairs, loveseats, ottomans, dining chairs with upholstered seats. When your lease ends, pull it off and fold it into a moving box — the original upholstery underneath is untouched, and the slipcover comes with you to the next place.
