8 No-Drill Bathroom Upgrades Every Renter Should Know

Better storage, better light. Your landlord won't notice a thing.

Most rental bathrooms come with a fixed mirror, a pedestal sink with zero counter space, and terrible overhead lighting. These eight upgrades use tension rods, adhesive hooks, over-door racks, and peel-and-stick products. Nothing that leaves a mark on move-out day.

← Back to all rooms
01

Add a tension rod corner shower caddy

The soap dish that came with your rental shower holds half a bar of soap and nothing else. A tension rod corner caddy wedges between the tub and ceiling, giving you two or three wire shelves for shampoo, conditioner, and a razor. Twist it tight. The rubber end caps grip tile without scratching. Measure your ceiling height before buying; most caddies fit standard 8-foot ceilings but taller bathrooms need an extension pole.

Tension holds it in place. Removes in ten seconds with no trace on tile or tub.
02

Slide a freestanding shelf over the toilet

An over-the-toilet shelving unit slips around the tank and stands on its own legs. No wall attachment, no hardware. Use it for spare toilet paper rolls, a small plant, rolled towels, or a basket of toiletries. Look for one with adjustable shelves so you can fit taller items on the bottom tier. The whole unit lifts away when you move.

Freestanding. Slides over the tank, doesn't touch the walls. Packs flat.
03

Cover the floor with peel-and-stick vinyl tiles

Rental bathroom floors tend to be builder-grade sheet vinyl or chipped ceramic. You can't replace them. Peel-and-stick vinyl tiles lay right on top with no adhesive beyond what's on the back of each tile. They're 12x12 inches, cut with a utility knife, and pull up clean when your lease ends. If the floor is your biggest complaint, go with two colors in a checkerboard pattern.

Lays on top of the existing floor. Peels up without residue.
04

Mount an adhesive shelf in the shower for small items

A small adhesive shelf on the shower wall keeps the little items (razor, face wash, loofah) nearby without eating up caddy space. Pick one with drainage slots so water doesn't pool under your products. Stick it at arm height, let the adhesive cure for 24 hours before putting anything on it, and keep the total weight under the shelf's rating, usually 6 to 10 pounds.

Adhesive peels off tile clean. No drill holes near plumbing.
05

Hang an over-the-door towel rack

Most rental bathrooms have one towel bar, maybe none. An over-the-door rack hangs from the top of the bathroom door with two metal brackets and gives you two to four bars for wet towels, a robe, or tomorrow's outfit. Get one with rubber-padded hooks so it doesn't clank when the door moves. Measure the gap between your door and the frame. You need about an inch of clearance for the door to close fully.

Hangs on the door, not the wall. Lifts off in two seconds.
06

Organize under the sink with stackable bins

The cabinet under a bathroom sink is usually a dark void with a pipe running through it. A couple of stackable open-front plastic bins turn it into usable zones: cleaning products in one, spare toilet paper in another, hair dryer and tools in a third. Make sure the bins are short enough to slide under the U-bend pipe. They just sit on the cabinet floor, no mounting needed.

Sits on the cabinet floor. Pulls out and moves with you.
07

Replace towel bars with adhesive hooks

Towel bars need screws, and towels on the floor get annoying. Large adhesive hooks rated for 7 to 10 pounds give you the same function with zero drilling. Space them about 18 inches apart so towels dry properly, and stick them on tile or smooth painted walls. Textured wallpaper and bare drywall won't hold. One hook per person, plus one by the sink for a hand towel.

Peels off tile without damage. No drywall repair.
08

Add a tension rod curtain to hide the ugly stuff

If your bathroom has a glass shower door or no divider at all, a tension rod with a curtain can screen the less attractive items: laundry hamper, toilet brush and plunger, open shelves with cleaning bottles. Run the rod across an alcove or between two walls. Use a lightweight cotton curtain so the rod doesn't sag. Nobody needs to see your toilet brush.

Tension rod between walls. No screws, no brackets, no trace.
