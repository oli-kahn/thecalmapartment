Grown-Up Guest Beds — 8 No-Drill Ways to Host Overnight Guests in a Rental

Someone stays over. They sleep on a real surface. You don't lose your deposit. Everyone wins.

Hosting overnight guests in a rental without a spare room can feel impossible. An air mattress on the living room floor that squeaks every time someone rolls over is not grown-up hosting. These eight options give your guests a real sleeping surface — and every single one requires zero drilling, zero wall mounting, and stores away when the visit is over.

01

Folding guest bed that packs flat into a closet

A folding guest bed with a trifold foam mattress or a collapsible metal frame opens in under a minute and stores upright in a closet. The trifold foam versions fold to roughly 4 inches deep by 25 inches wide and 75 inches tall — narrow enough to slide behind hanging clothes. The metal frame versions sit higher off the floor and feel closer to a real bed. Both types live in a closet corner 360 days a year and come out when someone stays over. No wall attachment, no assembly beyond unfolding.

Stands on its own feet or sits on the floor. Nothing touches the walls. Folds away completely between visits.
02

Daybed that doubles as a sofa during the day

A twin daybed with a backrest and two arms functions as a sofa when you push bolsters or large throw pillows against the back. At night you pull the pillows off and it's a standard twin bed with a real mattress — not a thin pad stretched over a frame. Place one in a home office, a corner of the living room, or a spare corner of the bedroom. It seats three during the day and sleeps one adult at night without anyone ending up on a couch cushion slope. Look for a daybed with a trundle underneath if you sometimes host couples.

A freestanding piece of furniture. No mounting, no drilling, no wall contact beyond maybe leaning against it.
03

Raised inflatable mattress that doesn't look like camping gear

A raised air mattress — the kind that inflates to 18 or 20 inches high with a built-in electric pump — doesn't look like you dragged it out of a tent bag. Dress it with a fitted sheet, a thin mattress topper for warmth and cushioning, and a duvet, and it passes for a low-profile real bed. The height is the difference: climbing into an 18-inch bed feels normal, while the 6-inch camping ones make a grown adult feel like they're sleeping on the floor. It deflates in three minutes and packs into a duffel-sized carry bag that fits on a closet shelf.

Inflates when needed, deflates and vanishes into a bag. Zero contact with walls or floors beyond sitting there for a night or two.
04

Rollaway bed that folds up vertically for storage

A rollaway bed has a folding metal frame with a thin but real mattress attached. When guests leave, you fold the whole unit vertically — mattress and frame as one piece — and roll it into a closet or behind a room divider. The caster wheels make it easy to move between rooms without lifting. The mattress on a rollaway is thinner than a standard bed but thicker than a camping pad, and the frame keeps it off the floor so air circulates underneath. Hotels have used these for decades for a reason. Add felt pads to the casters if you have hardwood floors.

Rolls into a closet in one folded piece. Casters with felt pads won't scratch floors. Nothing attaches to anything.
05

Sleeper chair — compact armchair that converts to a single bed

A sleeper chair looks like an oversized armchair in its closed position. Pull the seat forward and fold the back down and it becomes a single bed roughly 30 by 75 inches — snug for a tall adult but fine for a night or two. The foam mattress is built into the chair, so there's no separate mattress to store or inflate. Keep it in a living room corner or a home office as your reading chair, and it converts in under a minute when a friend stays over. It's the smallest footprint option that still gives your guest an actual mattress.

One piece of furniture that does two jobs. No wall attachment. No separate mattress to store. Moves like any armchair.
06

Floor cushions and Japanese futon that convert to guest bedding

A shikibuton — a Japanese cotton futon mattress about 3 inches thick — rolls out directly on the floor at night and rolls up into a closet in the morning. Pair it with three or four large floor cushions (zabuton) that serve as seating during the day and extra padding under the futon at night. The whole setup — futon plus cushions — stacks into a closet corner when guests leave. Air out the futon in direct sunlight every few uses; cotton absorbs body moisture and needs ventilation to stay fresh. A tatami mat underneath helps with airflow if you have the budget and floor space.

Rolls up and goes in a closet. Nothing attaches to anything. The floor is the only surface involved.
07

Cabinet bed — a freestanding Murphy-bed alternative

A cabinet bed is a freestanding piece of furniture that looks like a tall armoire or wardrobe when closed — roughly 24 inches deep, 40 inches wide, and 80 inches tall. Open the doors, fold down the platform, and it becomes a twin or full bed with a real mattress inside. Unlike a wall-mounted Murphy bed, a cabinet bed does not attach to anything. It stands on its own base platform or legs. The mattress folds inside the cabinet with the bedding still on it in some models. You place it against a wall but you never drill into it. It's the closest thing to a hidden guest bed that doesn't require landlord permission.

A freestanding furniture piece. Moves with you like a dresser would. Zero holes in walls, zero wall mounting.
08

Curtain divider to create a temporary guest zone in a studio

A floor-to-ceiling tension rod with blackout curtains hung from it creates a private guest sleeping zone in a studio apartment. The spring-loaded rod twists into place between floor and ceiling — no screws, just twist pressure — and the curtains slide across when your guest needs privacy. When nobody is staying over, push the curtains open and the room is whole again. If your ceiling is too high or textured for a tension rod to grip, use a freestanding folding screen with fabric panels instead. Either way, your guest gets a visual barrier between their sleeping area and the rest of the apartment.

Tension rod twists in and out with zero holes. Curtains pack flat when you move. A folding screen just stands there and folds up.
