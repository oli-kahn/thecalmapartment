8 No-Drill Ways to Stay Cool in a Rental

Summer cooling strategies that work in any apartment — zero landlord approval needed

Landlords don't let you install window AC units, mount ceiling fans, or swap out single-pane windows. But you can still drop the temperature in your living room by several degrees without touching a drill. These eight strategies use tension rods, water, airflow, and smart timing. Nothing permanent, nothing the landlord can object to.

01

Apply peel-and-stick reflective window film

Reflective window film bounces solar heat back outside before it enters the room. Cut it to size with scissors, spray the glass with soapy water, and smooth it on with a credit card. It sticks with water alone — no adhesive touching the glass. When the lease ends, lift a corner and peel the whole sheet off in one piece. The glass underneath looks exactly like it did before. A south-facing or west-facing window coated with reflective film can drop the room temperature behind it by 5 to 8 degrees during peak sun.

Sticks with water. Peels off in one sheet. Zero residue on the glass.
02

Set up a cross-breeze with two box fans

Place one box fan facing inward in a window on the cooler side of the apartment. Place another facing outward in a window on the warmer side. The inward fan pulls fresh air in; the outward fan pushes hot air out. Together they create a wind tunnel that drops the room temperature noticeably within ten minutes. Position the inward fan low in the window where cooler air pools, and the outward fan higher where warm air rises. No installation needed — both fans just sit on the windowsill.

Fans sit on the windowsill. No mounting, no drilling, no tools.
03

Mount bamboo or reed blinds with a tension rod

Bamboo or reed blinds mounted inside the window frame stop sunlight before it reaches the glass, so the heat never enters the room. A tension rod twists into place inside the frame — no screws, no brackets, no holes. Roll the blinds down during the hottest part of the day and roll them up when the sun moves on. The natural fibers block direct light while letting enough glow through that the room doesn't feel like a cave. A standard tension rod inside a window frame holds blinds up to about 6 pounds.

Tension rod twists in, twists out. The window frame stays untouched.
04

Run a cool-mist humidifier where you sit

A cool-mist humidifier sprays fine water vapor into the air. As the mist evaporates, it pulls heat from the room — the same principle as sweat cooling your skin. A small tabletop unit covers a living room and drops the perceived temperature by about 5 degrees. Fill the tank once a day, plug it in, and point the nozzle toward where you sit. It also helps with dry-air problems: static shocks, scratchy throat, and cracked wood furniture. Clean the tank weekly with vinegar to keep minerals from building up.

Sits on a table. Unplugs and moves to the next apartment in seconds.
05

Close blackout curtains during peak sun hours

Hang blackout curtains on a tension rod across the window. Close them from 3pm to 6pm when the afternoon sun hits hardest — those three hours are when windows turn a living room into a greenhouse. The curtains block both light and heat. The room stays darker and measurably cooler. Open them after 6pm when the sun angle drops and you want the evening light back. The tension rod twists into place and leaves no holes in the wall or window frame. Fold the curtains into a moving box when the lease ends.

Tension rod leaves zero holes. Curtains fold flat and come with you.
06

Use a portable evaporative cooler on the floor

A portable evaporative cooler sits on the floor and plugs into a standard outlet. Fill the water tank, turn it on, and a fan blows air across a wet filter. The evaporation cools the air stream by 10 to 15 degrees in dry climates. No hose, no window kit, no exhaust vent — it works fully self-contained. Wheels let you roll it between rooms. Works best when humidity stays below 50 percent. Above that, evaporation slows down and the cooling effect weakens. In a dry-summer region this rivals a window AC unit without any installation.

Rolls room to room. No window or wall contact. Takes one outlet.
07

Open interior doors, close sun-facing rooms

Open all interior doors to let air move freely through the apartment. Close the doors to rooms with sun-facing windows during peak heat hours — those rooms heat up fast and leak warmth into the rest of the space. If you have a hallway with a window at one end, prop the bedroom and living room doors open to create a straight airflow corridor from end to end. In a studio, open the bathroom door and run the exhaust fan to pull air across the whole unit. Costs nothing and takes thirty seconds to set up.

Zero cost. Zero tools. Just open and close doors.
08

Switch to linen sheets, a buckwheat pillow, and a cotton blanket

Polyester and microfiber bedding traps body heat and leaves you sweating by 2am. Swap to linen sheets, a buckwheat hull pillow, and a lightweight cotton blanket. Linen breathes and wicks moisture instead of holding it against your skin. Buckwheat hulls leave air gaps inside the pillow so heat doesn't build up under your head — the pillow stays cool to the touch all night. A cotton blanket adds a layer without the weight or heat-trapping of a duvet. All three pack into a single laundry bag when you move.

No installation. Everything fits in a laundry bag. Comes with you.
